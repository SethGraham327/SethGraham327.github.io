COVID-19 Household Spending Reallocation

Econometrics / Data Analysis

Overview

This empirical research paper investigates how the COVID-19 pandemic affected household spending allocation across discretionary and non-discretionary categories. The study examines the nature of precautionary savings, consumer liquidity constraints, and whether the observed reallocation of capital persisted into the post-pandemic recovery period.

View Full PDF Report

Objectives

  • Analyze how household consumption decisions reflect underlying risk preferences and liquidity constraints during macroeconomic shocks.
  • Determine if discretionary spending contracted disproportionately compared to essential spending.
  • Assess whether pandemic-induced changes in capital allocation fully reversed during the economic recovery in 2023.

Methodology

The study leveraged a robust econometric approach to isolate causal and associative effects:

  • Data Source: Utilized nationally representative data from Statistics Canada's Survey of Household Spending (SHS).
  • Empirical Framework: Implemented a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) framework comparing 2019 (pre-treatment baseline), 2021 (pandemic treatment), and 2023 (recovery).
  • Modeling: Estimated baseline, log-linear, and category-level fixed-effects models with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors.

Key Findings & Results

Stable Aggregate Levels

Aggregate household spending did not experience a large, uniform decline during the pandemic, likely due to fiscal transfers and forced savings stabilizing total consumption.

Significant Reallocation

The DiD framework revealed a statistically significant reallocation. Discretionary spending declined disproportionately (an estimated -$558 relative shift per category) compared to essential goods.

Incomplete Reversal

The recovery period in 2023 showed only a partial reversal of these effects. Discretionary categories did not fully converge back to their pre-pandemic relative levels.

Persistent Shift in Preferences

The data suggests that macroeconomic shocks can induce persistent, longer-lasting shifts in consumption composition rather than temporary fluctuations.

Conclusion

These findings contribute to the literature by demonstrating that extreme macroeconomic shocks alter long-term consumption composition. For policymakers and investors, understanding these compositional adjustments is critical for anticipating the distributional and financial consequences of future economic shocks.
