Forecasting Monthly Electricity Revenue

Data Science / Time Series Analysis • R

Overview

This empirical study develops statistical models to forecast monthly electricity revenue for the state of Florida. Because electricity demand exhibits strong seasonal patterns and is heavily influenced by long-term economic trends, reliable revenue forecasts are critical for infrastructure planning, pricing strategies, and energy supply management.

View Full PDF Report

Objectives

  • Process and clean decades of historical energy data (1990-2025) from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA).
  • Develop and evaluate multiple competing forecasting models, including ETS and SARIMA frameworks.
  • Generate highly reliable revenue forecasts for the year 2026.

Methodology

The analysis employed rigorous time series econometric techniques in R:

  • Data Processing: Aggregated sector-level revenue and addressed heteroskedasticity using Box-Cox variance stabilization.
  • Model Building: Evaluated both Exponential Smoothing with Trend and Seasonality (ETS) and Seasonal Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA) models.
  • Validation: Utilized a holdout validation design with 2025 reserved as the out-of-sample test period. Evaluated models based on RMSE, MAE, MSE, and MAPE.
  • Diagnostics: Assessed stationarity using ADF/KPSS tests and verified residual white noise using Ljung-Box testing and ACF/PACF visual diagnostics.

Key Findings & Results

Winning Model

The best-performing model was a SARIMA specification: ARIMA(3,1,0)(2,1,0)[12] applied to post-2010 data.

High Accuracy

The model achieved a remarkably low Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) of 3.2% on the 2025 holdout set.

2026 Forecast

Revenue is forecasted to maintain strong seasonality, peaking in July and August at approximately $3.5 billion.

Residual White Noise

Ljung-Box testing (p=0.1646) confirmed no statistically significant autocorrelation remained in the residuals.

Conclusion

The study demonstrated that seasonal time-series models (specifically SARIMA) provide an effective and interpretable approach for forecasting electricity revenue. By capturing both short-term autoregressive dynamics and recurring seasonal patterns, these models can support high-level decision-making within the energy sector.
