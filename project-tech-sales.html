Tech Sales Compensation Analysis

HR Analytics / Regression Modeling • R

Overview

Organizations increasingly rely on quantitative analysis to guide workforce decisions, particularly in compensation and performance management. This project analyzes a dataset of 12,130 software sales representatives to develop regression models predicting salary and identifying the most influential factors driving compensation inequalities.

Methodology

  • Model Development: Developed six increasingly refined OLS regression models, testing non-linear curvature and interaction effects.
  • Validation: Evaluated models using K-fold Cross Validation (k=10) and holdout sets to minimize Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and maximize generalization.
  • Data Preprocessing: Handled right-skewed salary data via log-transformation, and applied standard mean-centering to continuous predictors to mitigate multicollinearity.

Key Findings

Curvature in Experience

Earnings rise with Age and Net Promoter Score (NPS), but at diminishing rates (negative quadratic terms).

The College Premium

Holding a college degree is associated with a highly statistically significant 15.3% premium in compensation.

Gender Gap

Even after controlling for performance, experience, and education, women earned approximately 8.4% less than comparable men.

Personality Disparities

Analysts and Sentinels earned substantially less (13.8% and 14.9%, respectively) compared to baseline Explorers.

Managerial Recommendations

The final model serves as an equity-monitoring tool. The findings highlighted that gender and education gaps warrant review to ensure equitable pay practices. Furthermore, because certain personality profiles earn less after controlling for performance, management must ensure that role assignments or evaluation criteria do not unintentionally favour specific personality traits over tangible sales metrics.
